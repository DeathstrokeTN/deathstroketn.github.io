Understanding Conditions

Learn about common conditions, what symptoms to expect, and mistakes to avoid.

⚕️ Stroke Recovery (Post-Stroke Care)

📋 Symptoms to Expect

  • 🔹 Physical weakness on one side of the body
  • 🔹 Difficulty speaking or understanding speech (Aphasia)
  • 🔹 Emotional changes: sudden crying or laughing, frustration
  • 🔹 Fatigue and need for much more sleep

💡 What You Should Understand

The brain is healing. Recovery takes a long time, and progress can be slow. Their emotional outbursts are often a result of brain injury, not their true feelings towards you.

⚠️ Mistakes to Avoid

  • Avoid Doing everything for them. Let them do what they can to rebuild independence.
  • Avoid Speaking louder if they have aphasia. They can hear you, they just have trouble processing the words. Speak slowly and clearly instead.
  • Avoid Ignoring your own back health when helping them move.

🌧️ Depression

📋 Symptoms to Expect

  • 🔹 Loss of interest in daily activities and hobbies
  • 🔹 Persistent sadness, "empty" mood, or irritability
  • 🔹 Significant changes in appetite and sleep patterns
  • 🔹 Lack of energy, chronic fatigue

💡 What You Should Understand

Depression is a medical illness, not a choice or a sign of weakness. They cannot simply "snap out of it." Small tasks like showering or eating can feel like climbing a mountain to them.

⚠️ Mistakes to Avoid

  • Avoid Saying "cheer up" or "other people have it worse." This increases guilt.
  • Avoid Taking their isolation personally.
  • Avoid Forcing them into social situations before they are ready.

🌪️ Anxiety Disorder

📋 Symptoms to Expect

  • 🔹 Constant worrying and feeling restless
  • 🔹 Physical symptoms: racing heart, sweating, trembling
  • 🔹 Panic attacks (sudden intense fear)
  • 🔹 Avoidance of certain places or situations

💡 What You Should Understand

To the patient, the threat feels 100% real, even if it seems irrational to you. Their nervous system is caught in a "fight or flight" response.

⚠️ Mistakes to Avoid

  • Avoid Saying "just calm down" or "there is nothing to worry about."
  • Avoid Getting angry or frustrated when they ask for reassurance constantly.
  • Avoid Enabling their avoidance entirely (though pushing too hard is also bad).
